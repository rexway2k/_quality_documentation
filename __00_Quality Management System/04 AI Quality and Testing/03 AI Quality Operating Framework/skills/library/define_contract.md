# L1.S1 — Define Contract

## Purpose

Define the explicit expectations between the producer of work and the consumer of work so that the output can be built, judged, and verified against a known contract rather than assumptions.

## Governing Basis

Quality is the degree to which output matches the expectations of its consumers. Contracts exist whether they are named or not, and unnamed contracts are a primary source of failure. If the contract cannot be named, the system is not understood well enough to build correctly.

## When to Use

Use this skill at the start of any task, deliverable, decision, request, or dependency where one party is producing something another party will consume. This applies to documents, code, plans, prompts, decisions, reviews, analyses, and any other relational work.

## Inputs Required

- The request, task, or work item to be performed
- The known or likely consumer of the output
- Any stated expectations, goals, or constraints already available

## Process

1. Identify the producer and the consumer of the work. If there are downstream consumers beyond the immediate requester, identify them too.
2. State the expected outcome in explicit terms. Define what must be true when the work is complete.
3. Name the expectations that define success. These may be functional, structural, quality-related, timing-related, or contextual.
4. Convert implicit expectations into explicit statements. If an assumption is being made, surface it and attach it to the contract instead of leaving it hidden.
5. Check whether the contract is specific enough to support verification. If verification cannot be defined, the contract is incomplete.
6. Confirm whether any unresolved dependency or unknown affects the contract. If so, record its scope and impact explicitly.

## Outputs Produced

- A clear statement of the producer-consumer contract
- A list of known consumers and downstream consumers
- A defined expected outcome
- A set of explicit expectations and constraints attached to the work
- A visible list of assumptions or open items affecting the contract

## Failure Signals

- The consumer cannot be named clearly.
- Success is described vaguely or only in subjective terms.
- Expectations remain implicit or contradictory.
- Verification cannot be defined from the contract.  
- Work begins before outcome and expectations are explicit.

## Escalation Rule

If the contract cannot be made explicit enough to define outcome, constraints, and verification, do not continue as if the work is well understood. Either reduce the work to a bounded interim contract or pause and surface the missing contract elements explicitly.

## Completion Criteria

This skill is complete when the work can answer:

- What is expected?
- Who is the consumer?
- What defines success?
- What assumptions are in play?
- How will fulfillment of the contract be verified?
