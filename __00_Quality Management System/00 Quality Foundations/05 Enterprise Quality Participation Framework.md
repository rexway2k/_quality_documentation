# Enterprise Quality Ownership and Participation Framework

## Purpose

This framework provides a starting point for delivery teams to discuss, document, and align quality responsibilities throughout the solution delivery lifecycle.

The purpose of this framework is not to prescribe a single delivery model or ownership structure.

Instead, it is intended to help teams evaluate quality-related activities, determine whether those activities should occur within their delivery process, and identify who is responsible for execution, collaboration, consultation, and awareness.

Every delivery team is unique. Teams may choose to modify, remove, combine, or expand activities based on the needs of their business area, technology stack, release process, and delivery methodology.

This framework should be viewed as a conversation starter rather than a compliance checklist.

Teams should regularly review this framework and ask:

- Should this activity exist?
- Does it add value?
- Is ownership clear?
- Are expectations understood?
- Are risks appropriately managed?
- Are quality activities occurring early enough?

The goal is to improve quality outcomes through clarity, collaboration, shared accountability, and continuous improvement.

---

## Relationship to the Enterprise Quality Engagement Model

This framework supplements the Enterprise Quality Engagement Model by providing examples of activities that commonly occur throughout solution delivery.

The Enterprise Quality Engagement Model defines when and why quality professionals engage in delivery activities.

This framework helps teams define who participates, contributes, and owns specific activities.

Together these documents support:

- Shared ownership of quality
- Clear expectations
- Proactive risk management
- Effective collaboration
- Continuous improvement
- Delivery confidence

---

## Participation Definitions

### Responsible (R)

Actively performs or executes the activity.

### Accountable (A)

Owns the outcome and ensures the activity occurs successfully.

### Consulted (C)

Provides input, expertise, feedback, guidance, or review.

### Informed (I)

Receives awareness of the activity, outcome, or decision.

---

## Important Guiding Principles

Quality professionals do not own quality.

Quality professionals help teams establish confidence, identify risks, improve delivery practices, and validate expectations.

Many activities within this framework may show Quality Assurance (QA) and Quality Engineering (QE) professionals as Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, or Informed depending upon team structures and delivery practices.

The correct answer is not always the same across every team.

The most important outcome is that ownership is clear, expectations are understood, and quality remains a shared responsibility across the delivery team.

# Core Quality Ownership Areas

While quality is a shared responsibility across the delivery team, Quality Assurance (QA) and Quality Engineering (QE) professionals maintain explicit ownership for several quality activities.

These activities should not be assumed to occur automatically.

Quality professionals are expected to drive, coordinate, facilitate, or directly perform these activities as appropriate for their team.

Ownership does not necessarily imply sole execution.

Ownership means ensuring the activity occurs and achieves its intended outcome.

# Risk Assessment Guidance

Quality professionals should participate in risk assessment activities throughout the solution delivery lifecycle.

Risk assessments should consider factors such as:

- Business impact
- User impact
- Technical complexity
- Security concerns
- Compliance requirements
- Integration dependencies
- Operational impact
- Deployment complexity

Example Risk Scoring:

| Score | Classification |
|---------|---------|
| 1 – 3 | Low |
| 4 – 6 | Medium |
| 8 – 12 | High |
| 15 – 25 | Critical |

Risk thresholds and governance requirements should follow established team, department, and organizational standards.

Risk scores should be reviewed whenever:

- Significant requirements change
- New functionality is introduced
- Architecture changes occur
- New dependencies are identified
- Production incidents reveal previously unidentified risks

# Success Criteria

The objective of quality participation is not to create additional process or approvals.

Successful quality participation should result in:

- Clear expectations
- Shared understanding
- Early risk identification
- Better planning decisions
- Improved validation effectiveness
- Stronger release confidence
- Reduced production incidents
- Improved customer outcomes
- Increased automation effectiveness
- Continuous improvement of products, processes, and delivery practices

Quality is not owned by a single role, team, or phase.

Quality is a shared commitment to delivering outcomes that meet expectations.
